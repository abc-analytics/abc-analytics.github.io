  • Mathematical Statistics and Data Analysis
  • John Rice
  • The author is clearly knowledgeable in both theory and practice, but the book is one of those that include a wide range of topics and frequently drift from one to another, rather than a focused and pedagogical textbook for self-study. Its discussions range from basic to fairely advanced, including Cauchy pdf, Copula, Improper Priors, Bootstrap, Poisson Dispersion Test, Hanging Rootogram, Gibbs Sampling, Rao-Cramer bounds, etc. Reader already familar with the topics should appreciate and find depth in his discussions; on the other hand, reader without prior knowledge will likely be confused and overwhelmed. The author includes many graphs of distributions and gives practical advice for complex computations. Should review later.

  • Introduction To Probability
  • Dimitri Bertsekas
  • Best text on introductory probability focusing on a few fundamental ideas using intuition and just-necessary (and readily understandable) mathematics. Unlike the more abstract style of Kai Lai Chung, its style is concise and easy to follow and encourages understanding of principles. The way he introduces hypothesis testing is fresh and effective, unlike the standardized procedure-listing style of many texts. The chapter on Markov Chains is brief and focuses on definitions and big picture rather than any computations (no matrices even used).

  • Introduction to Mathematical Statistics and its Applications
  • Richard Larsen
  • A good first step into the long journey of learning statistics, with both chatty discussions to hold the reader's hands (but not so chatty as to irritate) and just-enough mathematics (basic calculus) to prove statements, such that reader should have an idea about the motivation and assumptions behind commonly used tests.

    The materials are comprehensive and focus on 1-2 variables so do not require matrix algebra. It appropriately introduces concepts of random variables and common pdfs (not so much on mgfs) then to hypothesis testing and derivations of chi (gamma) / F (ratio of chi) / t (ratio of z and chi) sampling distributions. However, it doesn't (or is not intended to) cover regressions well and its ANOVA discussion is more difficult to follow than Ross.

    Solutions manual.
