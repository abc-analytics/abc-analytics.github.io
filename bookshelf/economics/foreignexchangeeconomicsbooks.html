  • Tragedy of the Euro
  • Philipp Bagus
  • Short, concise, and nontechnical on the basics of the EMU plus explanations of general concepts. The discussion is fact-based and clear including key historical and political backgrounds especially on Germany and France. I like its chapter on Fed-ECB comparison where he describes how the ECB accepts a wider range of bonds and prefers repos in open market operations versus outright bond transactions by the Fed (I guess due to the variety of EU government bonds). When discussing money creation, he should have mentioned that banks take on credit risks and are subject to capital requirements so are not as simple a money printer as portrayed. However, he does a good job outlining the essence of government financing and central banking in general as well as the structural imbalances and convoluted incentives within EMU (with the Greek episode as a case in point) which suggest that EMU is hardly an optimal currency area.

  • Exorbitant Privilege
  • Barry Eichengreen
  • A longwinded talk on the USD's exorbitant(=unfair) privilege in today's monetary system and how the US benefits from lower interest rates and the accompanying leverages. Despite potential rivals such as EUR/gold/SDRs/CNY, the author reminds the USD remains the least dirty shirt with incumbency advantages. However, I dislike the details on politicians which I find excessive and irrelevant. Interesting to note the author's prescient scenario that US and China would be involved in disputes over trade and FX which are seen to cause trade imbalances and US unemployment, which would likely lead to tariffs; that is exactly what is happening under Trump administration now in Q2 2019!
